B. Database Administrator - A day in the life

Careers

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yAzCBH_o7OE

David Finnigan(Database Administrator / ANZ)

Q1:What kinds of things do you do in a normal day?

DF: There are two things I do in a normal day – the first is to make sure that the databases I support are running quickly and that the appropriate people have access. I also make sure that any overnight jobs such as backups or reports have finished successfully. And then the other thing that we look after is a lot of consultation with projects that might be intending to use databases in the future.

Q2:Why are databases important?

DF: I think databases are critical to a lot of modern organisations. It’s probably not too big a stretch to say that no database, no oraganisation or no company. And as such a DBA has a large role to play if a database system is down or even running slowly that can cause a big impact to customers and internal staff within an organisation, and as a result a DBA can make a really positive difference in truning that around and repairing it.

Q3:What’s your favourite part of your job?

DF: I think repairing issues, addressing those sort of problems are one of my favourite parts; when something has gone wrong and it’s impacting a lot of people, it’s very rewarding to be able to apply to your knowledge and skills and experience, and resolve that issue quickly. And to oftentimes literally hear the relief in the users’s voices when your’re able to tell them that it’s working properly again.

Q4: How did you become a DBA

DF: I started in an IT support role like a lot of people do, and in the organisation I was working at that time they didn’t have a dedicated DBA so I sort of organically moved into that area. The need was there and then I’d go to training courses and eventually just chose to specialise.

• What kind of work is done by the IT professional?

Generally speaking, computer and information system managers implement and supervise computer-related activities within a company or organization. They work with senior management to help determine the company's needs for computing systems, and then develop plans to meet those needs.

In order to effectively complete this work, computer and information system managers must understand the latest developments and trends in computing systems, including network security. As cyber attacks become more frequent and complex, this problem has become more common in organizations. more and more important.

• What kinds of people does the IT professional interact with? Are they other IT professionals? Clients? Investors? The general public?

Computer and information systems managers direct the work of other IT professionals, such as computer software engineers and computer programmers, computer system analysts and computer support specialists. They plan and coordinate activities such as installing and upgrading hardware and software, programming and system design, the realization of computer networks, and the development of Internet and Intranet sites. They are increasingly involved in the maintenance, maintenance and security of the network. They analyze the organization's computer and information needs from an operational and strategic perspective, and determine immediate and long-term personnel and equipment requirements.

Most of them are other IT professionals.

• Where does the IT professional spend most of their time?

Computer and information system managers spend the majority of their time in an office, work a minimum of 40 hours a week, and often work evening and weekends to resolve disputes and meet deadlines.
